# Category 4: Strategic Concepts

## The Standard Style — Core Framework
> Source: The Standard Style (307th, Prismata Library, Dec 2017)

The "standard style" is the baseline competitive framework. Six principles:

### Principle 1: Buy, Buy, Buy
Keep resources low by buying as much as you can each turn. Focus on Drones early, attackers later. Both have concrete effects each turn — Drones give gold, attackers deal damage. Prismata is a game of exponential growth; getting investments out one turn earlier snowballs quickly.

"Every resource you spend on an investment, like a Drone or an attacker, will pay for itself quickly, and after that will only gain more and more value."

**Energy exception**: Energy is only important early (for Drones). In mid-to-late game, it's fine to waste Energy.

**Float rule of thumb**: 1-2 Gold or Green leftover is fine. Avoid floating 3+.

### Principle 2: Live on the Edge
Put off defense until the last minute. There's no advantage to buying defense early — blockers do nothing until you need them. Build only enough Prompt defense to clear the breach warning, then put everything else into Drones or attackers.

"Most defense in Prismata is Prompt, and since the game displays the maximum damage your opponent can do next turn, as long as you buy enough Prompt defense to make that breach warning sign go away, you're fine."

**Non-prompt blockers**: Higher HP to compensate for build time. More cost-efficient. Plan ahead for when they finish construction.

**Critical warning**: "Even though cutting your defense close is good, actually allowing a breach is very bad. When you get breached, you can't absorb any of your opponent's attack, meaning that you take more damage, and on top of that they get to target your most vulnerable backline units."

### Principle 3: Absorb is Awesome
Go for the biggest blocker in the set and absorb on it. Absorb is the primary defender's advantage in Prismata (analogous to distance in StarCraft).

"Making two Steelsplitters costs 12BB and does two damage per turn. A Wall can absorb two damage per turn and costs 5B. So, in this case, absorb is over twice as efficient as attack."

**Only one absorber per turn**: Due to how damage assignment works, you can only absorb on one unit per turn. Get the biggest one possible.

### Principle 4: Changing Gears
Prismata has distinct phases because of absorb. Once you're absorbing maximum damage per turn, there's no more defender's advantage — switch from Drones to attackers.

"Once you're over the absorb barrier and actually doing damage, attackers take priority over Drones because of how high value they are."

### Principle 5: How Many Drones?
Base it off the absorb barrier:
- Wall absorb (2): ~12-15 Drones, stay on 2 Engineers
- 4-health absorber: ~20 Drones, go to 3 Engineers
- 5+ health absorber: 20+ Drones, definitely 3rd Engineer

"If the biggest absorber has 4 health, use your own judgement — if the rest of the set seems aggressive, stay on two Engineers; if it seems defensive, go up to three."

### Principle 6: Setting Up Tech
Don't overtech. Rules of thumb for tech resource spending:
- 1 Conduit = 3 Gold/turn to spend
- 1 Blastforge = 5 Gold/turn to spend
- 1 Animus = 8 Gold/turn to spend

"Your tech setup will need to let you get your absorber out, while also making both attack and defense."

## Defense Theory
> Source: Defense Concepts (wiki)

### Absorb
"Absorb is the strongest, most busted mechanic in the game, and you want to be taking advantage of it as often as possible." — Will Ma, Lunarch Studios Founder

Non-fragile units heal to full after surviving damage. Wall with 3 HP takes 2 damage → heals to 3. The 2 damage is effectively meaningless.

**Block ordering**: With mixed fragile/non-fragile defense, put fragile units first. Example: 7 damage with Aegis (fragile 5HP) and Wall (3HP) — Aegis takes 5 lethal, Wall absorbs remaining 2. Reverse order wastes absorb potential.

### Soak
Defense that dies. Blockers sacrificed to take lethal damage. Non-fragile units CAN soak but it's inefficient — purpose-built fragile blockers (Forcefield, Aegis) are better soakers.

### Defensive Granularity
The ability to block ANY specific damage amount while maximizing absorb. Varied HP values across blockers prevent opponents from "exploiting" (attacking for amounts that deny absorb).

**Classic example**: 2 Walls alone = poor granularity (opponent attacks for 3, 4, or 5 and you lose 3 defense regardless). 1 Wall + 3 Engineers = good granularity (each damage increment costs you differently).

**Commentary angle**: "An opponent with 5 Militia can attack with 3 and click 2 for gold — exploiting the lack of granularity."

### Preventive Defense (Non-Prompt)
Non-prompt blockers are cheaper per HP than prompt ones:
- Infusion Grid (6HP, 7G) vs Wall (3HP, 5G+1B)
- Perforator vs Rhino: 2G cheaper
- Shredder vs Wall: more HP + attack threat

"Their damage potential will count toward your attack total, complicating your opponent's defense."

## Threat Types
> Source: Beginner's strategy guide: defending threat or breachproof (wiki)

**Threat** = the number next to the sword symbol. Sum of all opponent attacking values. If your defense > threat, you're safe from breach.

But threat is not always accurate:

- **Absorber threat**: Some absorbers can click for damage (e.g., Omega Splitter). Usually they want to absorb, but you must defend against the possibility they attack instead.
- **Fake freeze threat**: Excessive chill that can't all be applied usefully.
- **Dying threat**: Attacking units that will die to YOUR attack on YOUR turn. If you can guarantee killing them, their threat is temporary.
- **Fake threat**: Units that can't actually attack. "If a single Animus powers 3 Perforators, then one of them will not be able to attack — 1 of this threat is fake."

**Overdefending**: Spending more on defense than needed. "This is usually a very bad thing, as the resources you have spent overdefending could be better used by growing your economy or buying attackers."

## Breachproof Strategy
> Source: Defense Strategies (wiki) + Beginner's guide (wiki) + Prismata Library (307th)

"Breachproof is a unique strategy that plays completely differently from how I advocated playing in The Standard Style. When playing breachproof, you make your entire side resilient, so that you can safely allow a breach." — 307th

### Core Concept
Skip traditional defense entirely. Mass high-HP green attackers (Gauss Cannons, Iso Kronuses, Venge Cannons) backed by alternative economy (Trinity Drones, Thorium Dynamos). Opponent breaches you every turn, but damage is minimal because all units have massive HP.

### Types of Breachproof
> Source: Breachproof series (307th, Prismata Library)

1. **Full Breachproof**: All-in from the start. No Blastforge, no blockers.
2. **Semi-Breachproof**: Defend normally initially, then transition to BP when defense becomes unsustainable.
3. **Venge Cannon All-In**: Convert as many Drones as possible into Venge Cannons in a single explosive turn.
4. **Iceblade Golem All-In**: High threat from freeze ability makes it hard to defend against.

### Countering Breachproof
> Source: Beginner's guide (wiki)

"Focus on non-BP units (Tarsiers, Steelsplitters) and your opponent's economy: Drones, or advanced BP Drones like Trinity Drones. It is tempting to go for the BP attacking units like Gauss Cannon to reduce pressure, but it will simply let your opponent build more of them."

### Rock, Paper, Scissors Dynamic
> Source: Breachproof part 3 (307th)

"Breachproof is mostly used as a counter to something your opponent does — often a heavy commitment to Chill or big red."

## Tempo vs Economy
> Source: Tips (wiki)

"A useful criterion is whether strategies aim for a short-term advantage (to end the game quickly) or a long-term one (to overwhelm your opponent eventually)."

**Tempo (aggressive)**: Rush attackers early, force defensive response, end game before opponent's economy scales.

**Economy (defensive)**: Build Drones, get big absorber, outlast opponent with superior late-game resources.

"Choosing to go for the tempo play or the econ play is a matter of both experience and taste."

## Set Reading (6-Step Framework)
> Source: 307th — "Introduction to Set Reading" (prismatalibrary.blog, Jan 2018) + Tips (wiki)

"This is the most important advice of all. It is the key allowing one to take advantages, win games, and improve in the long run." "Set reading is a skill with no ceiling."

**The 6-Step Method:**

1. **Identify the Best Absorber**: "The best absorber is the closest to a sure thing that you'll get in Prismata — it's almost always a good idea." This determines your defensive foundation and cascades into all subsequent decisions.

2. **Determine Economy Size**: "The bigger the defender's advantage, the bigger an economy you want before you switch to making attack." Strong absorbers warrant larger drone counts.

3. **Evaluate Tech Requirements**: Calculate resource costs for supporting your absorber, then assess remaining economy for additional technology.

4. **Plan Offensive Units**: Select attackers compatible with your tech setup. Random-set attackers are prioritized over base units due to superior power.

5. **Address Soak Needs**: Plan secondary defenses beyond absorption. "Most absorbers require at least one Blue, so you'll have a Blastforge, and Blastforges can make Walls for soak."

6. **Select Opening Build Order**: Choose an opening aligned with your complete gameplan, adjusted for player position (P1 or P2).

See [06-meta-expert.md](06-meta-expert.md) for 8 worked Set Reading Saturday examples by 307th.

## Key Units vs Support Units
> Source: Tips (wiki)

"Key units in Prismata are the backbone of whole strategies. Not all units were designed equal."

**Key units** = win conditions. Examples: big absorbers, powerful legendary attackers, dominant tech units.

**Support units** = assist execution, protect key units. Examples: cheap defenders, tech fixers, economy boosters.

"The trick to devising a strategy in Prismata is to go for key units in the most efficient way available while getting the right support."

"The distinction between support and key units is not always clear. Omega Splitter is both key and support."

## Rush Theory
> Source: Prismata Blog — "Balancing Prismata Openings Through Unit Design" series + AI-synthesized "Developer Facts"

**Fact 1**: When economies are small, buying attackers first is a losing play — opponent absorbs the damage and out-scales.

**Fact 2**: When economies are large, buying attackers first is a winning play.

**Fact 3**: The later the game goes, the more advantageous it is to be the "rusher" (first to attack).

**Fact 5**: The optimal time to commit to a rush is "as soon as it's not a losing play." Wait too long and the opponent rushes first.

## Syncing Exhaust Attackers
> Source: Tips (wiki)

Exhaust attackers (Iso Kronus, Scorchilla) alternate between attacking and resting. If you buy two unsynchronized, they attack on different turns — half the threat each turn instead of concentrated burst.

"You want to combine exhaust attackers with preventive defense. Since you can anticipate turns in advance, buy non-Prompt defense like Infusion Grid."

---

## Offensive Finesse
> Source: Tips (wiki) + Defense Concepts (wiki)

Using flexible attackers (Steelsplitter, Militia) to exploit lack of granularity. You choose each turn whether to click (attack) or hold back (block/produce gold), forcing opponent to guess your actual damage.

"An attacking opponent can take advantage of a defense with poor granularity."

---

## "Breach Me If You Dare" Gambit
> Source: Defense Strategies (wiki)

Intentionally allowing a small breach to free up resources. Works when breach damage is less than main attackers' HP, or when opponent must invest heavily (e.g., using many Frostbites) to achieve the breach.

"The point of this bet is either to lure your opponent into making a mistake, or simply to free up resources."

---

## Full Frontline Strategy
> Source: Defense Strategies (wiki)

"Units with Frontline have more Health per gold cost than their backline counterparts. They also never have Fragile." When multiple frontline units are in the set (Wild Drone + Iceblade Golem or Shredder), rushing frontline units forgoes the need to block.

## Yujiri's Extended Strategy Theory
> Source: Yujiri (former #2 ranked player, ~2100 rating) — "The Basics of Prismata" + "Tips" (yujiri.xyz, archived via Wayback Machine)

### Pressure Bonus
"The more damage you have, the better additional damage is." When an opponent must buy defense, they exhaust efficient sources (Walls) first, then turn to less efficient options (Rhinos, holding Drones). Concentrated burst damage forces inefficient defense responses.

**Implication**: Buy pressure units (Scorchilla, Plasmafier, Cluster Bolt) on turns when the enemy is already scrambling for defense. "Pressure units are balanced so that they're weak when they force out efficient defense but strong when they force out inefficient defense."

### Pressure Beats Red
"Since red defense is so inefficient to compensate for red attack being so efficient, pressure units tend to do better against red strategies. This is especially true against double-Animus units. Double-Animus units are the epitome of greed, so pressure units are the textbook counter."

### Deny Absorb (Expanded)
Multiple ways to deny absorb beyond exploiting granularity:
1. **Buildtime attackers first** — "The longer you don't deal damage, the longer before the opponent can start absorbing." Tarsier (buildtime 2) denies absorb for an extra turn.
2. **Don't rush the second attacker** — "If you have 1 attacker and the enemy has a Wall, a 2nd attacker does nothing until the 3rd arrives."
3. **Lancetooth / cost-attack units** — "If you have 2 Tarsiers and the opponent buys a Wall, Lancetooth becomes insanely good because they already have the Wall ready and waiting to absorb, so the 2 damage it costs is worth literally 0."

### Vigilance (Counter to Threat)
> Source: Yujiri — Tips

"The best counter to threat is vigilant defense — units that block while doing something else useful."

**Key vigilant units**: Xeno Guardian, Urban Sentry, Borehole Patroller, Ossified Drone, plus energy-using units (Cauterizer, Electrovore, Galvani Drone) since Engineers can block AND produce energy.

"Building an army of Xeno Guardians is usually a mistake, but against threat, you should absolutely make an army of them."

**Rhino as vigilance**: "Rhino is a bad purchase for prompt defense and a bad purchase for just an attacker, but if it can defend against threat on its prompt turn, survive, then go on to attack and block 2 real damage later, it's pretty good."

### Gambiting (Intentional Small Breach)
> Source: Yujiri — Tips

"Sometimes you'll see very skilled players purposefully allow a small breach when playing against threat, even though it would've been easy to prevent. This is a gambit."

**When to gambit**: Against mass Frostbites: "If they lose all five Frostbites, it's like giving you 15 free defense next turn." Against Drake threat: "They'd have to lose their Wall to save the Rhino AND give up a Blastforge through the Drake."

### Hold Drone vs Forcefield (Against Threat)
"Against threat, since your Drone won't die if you hold it, buying a Forcefield is the same as holding a Drone except for one difference: the Forcefield loses the option of returning the Drone to work later." Hold the Drone first; only Forcefield when you're certain you need the defense.

### Don't Waste Attack
"A common new player instinct is to blindly attack with everything, but if your opponent has a Wall and you have nothing except two Militias, attacking does nothing (Wall absorbs it) and you could have got 2 gold instead."

"Even with 3 Militias you shouldn't attack into a Wall (because 3 gold is worth more than the 1 Engineer you'd kill)."

### Attackers Are Better Than Drones
> Source: Yujiri — Basics

"When they're not being absorbed, attackers are straight-up more efficient than Drones; the only reason you buy Drones at first is because early attackers are susceptible to being blunted by a Wall."

"If you waited until turn 3 or so and got 2 Blastforges for 2 Steelsplitters a turn, you only lose one turn of production to absorb. The advantage of waiting is clear. But if you wait too long, your opponent will be the first to start profitably making attackers."

### Blocking with Lifespan Units
"Attacker-defenders with lifespan are almost always better off blocking than attacking on their last turn. Doomed Mech can either give you 2 attack or 5 defense on its last turn."

### Overdefend to Save Key Attackers
"If you have Borehole Patrollers blocking, buy enough defense so you don't have to lose them. Borehole is worth much more as an attacker than as 2 defense."

### Verify the Damage Indicator
"The attack indicator assumes the opponent has enough resources to click all their stuff. If they have a Zemora Voidbringer but not enough green to fire it, you can defend for 8 less than the indicator says."

"It also assumes all chill will be put to use without overflow."

## Phases of a Prismata Game
> Source: Strategy Guide wiki (307th, adapted from Reddit post) — archived via Wayback Machine

### 1. Economy Phase
Build Drones. "Look for the biggest absorber to determine how greedy to get." Consider: Can the absorber get bought? Is it countered? (e.g., Defense Grid countered by Tatsu Nullifier). Zemora/Savior bought in this phase.

### 2. Transition Phase
"Still building economy, but setting up tech for later phases. Common to get a little bit of attack to force out a defender." Can take 1-2 damage without absorbing if it helps the build. Gauss Fabricator/Resophore bought here.

### 3. Attack and Absorb Phase
"Build your biggest absorber and pump out as much attack as you can. Don't build Centurion until it's absorbing at least 4 damage." Focus on long-term attackers (Tarsier, Tantalum Ray).

### 4. Attack and Defense Phase
"Once opponent has more damage than you can absorb, start building defense. Still building attack with leftover resources." Transition to short/medium-term attackers (Rhino, Tia Thurnax, Plasmafier, Grimbotch). Get a Conduit for Forcefields.

### 5. Defense Phase
"Opponent's attack is so high that you devote all resources to not getting breached. Hang on and hope your opponent breaks first!"

### 6. Breach(proof) Phase
"One or both sides eventually get breached. Some sets allow transition to breachproof — although they lose absorb and some damage each turn, their high-HP units keep them in the game."

## Mathematical Framework
> Source: Yujiri — "Intro to the Math Behind Prismata" (yujiri.xyz, archived)

### Resource Values
Derived from tech building balance (each produces 1/3 of cost per turn):
- **Gold** = 1.00 (baseline)
- **Green** = 1.33 (1/3 of Conduit's 4 cost)
- **Blue** = 1.67 (1/3 of Blastforge's 5 cost)
- **Red** = 1.00 (Animus costs 6, produces 2 red)
- **Energy** ≈ 0.56

### Inflation
"Inflation refers to things being worth more now than later." Standard Drone inflation = **1.33x** (9 gold now → 12 gold worth next turn). Each unit takes 3 turns to pay for itself.

Formula: `(cost + production_per_turn) / cost`

"The more efficient the units, the higher the inflation." This is why in high-econ games with big absorbers, the game is slower (lower inflation) and non-permanent units are relatively better.

### Barrier Value (BV)
Wall costs 6.67 effective, gives 3 BV → **1 BV ≈ 2.22 gold**. Mixed with Engineers for granularity → **approximate BV = 2.3** in base set.

### Why Attackers Beat Drones
Tarsier recursive inflation = **1.39** > Drone inflation = **1.33**. "When not absorbed, attackers are a better investment than economy." The absorb barrier is the hurdle — once past it, stop buying Drones.

### Key Equilibrium Points
- Tarsier/Rhino equilibrium: **1.50** (above = Rhinos better, below = Tarsiers better)
- Wall/Forcefield equilibrium: **1.47**
- Gauss Cannon/Tarsier equilibrium: **1.47**

## Chill Theory (Complete Framework)
> Source: 307th — Chill series (6 parts), prismatalibrary.blog, Jan 2018

### Why Chill is Good (Part I)
Chill units prevent opponent units from blocking, providing strategic value despite dealing no direct damage. Four primary uses:

1. **Absorb Denial**: Freezing the opponent's strongest absorber reduces defensive capacity, effectively increasing damage output.
2. **Exploits**: Freezing blockers disrupts granularity, creating gaps that reduce overall absorption potential.
3. **Eliminating Doomed Units**: "You can freeze lifespan 1 units...it won't soak up any damage" — converting blocked attack into real damage (e.g., a frozen Doomed Mech worth 5 damage).
4. **Threat Creation**: The most significant application. "Chill is guaranteed to either do damage to your opponent (by breaching) or pressure your opponent (by forcing out defense)." If opponents add defense, you simply don't activate chill — they wasted resources regardless.

### Types of Chill (Part II)
Categorized primarily by **activation cost (AC)** — the resource expense to use the ability.

**Low-AC Units** (repeatedly activated cheaply): Cryo Ray, Shiver Yeti, Tatsu Nullifier, Iceblade Golem, Nivo Charge, Vai Mauronax. "Low-AC units are better at getting value through denying absorb, exploiting, and freezing doomed units."

**High-AC Units** (significant commitment per activation): Frostbite (sacrifices itself), Frost Brooder (produces Frostbites with lifespan 3), Endotherm Kit (produces both Frostbites and Cryo Rays). High-AC units excel at threat generation through concentrated chill damage.

### Defending Against Chill: The Easy Bits (Part III)
Three basic defensive strategies:

1. **Against Absorb Denial**: Maintain backup absorbers. "You can't stop your opponent from freezing your biggest absorber. What you can do, though, is have a backup absorber."
2. **Against Exploits**: "To counter this, make more Engineers." Build redundancy in blocking units.
3. **Against Freezing Doomed Units**: Delay doomed units by holding them at higher lifespan values. Produce numerous doomed units to exhaust limited freeze supply (fails against unlimited-freeze units like Shiver Yeti).

### Defending Against Threat with Gambits (Part IV)
A **gambit** is allowing a small breach rather than fully defending against chill threat. Uses "divide and choose" dynamics — the goal is to make both opponent options (breach or hold threat) roughly equivalent in value, making their best option as weak as possible.

Defense amount depends on:
- **Breach cost**: High-value units (Centurion, Defense Grid) require fuller defense
- **Absorber size**: Larger absorbers justify more gambitting
- **Unit vulnerability**: 1-health attackers (Tarsiers) increase breach cost for opponent
- **Chill type**: Frostbites are better to gambit against than 3-charge Cryo Rays

"Start with small gambits until comfortable with the strategy."

### Defending Against Threat with Vigilance (Part V)
**Vigilant units** can block while simultaneously performing another useful function (attacking, generating resources). "Units that can block, while also doing something else, like attacking or making Gold, are said to have 'vigilance.'"

Vigilant units create a no-win scenario for opponents: if they hold chill, the vigilant unit keeps dealing damage; if they use chill, the soak trade is efficient for the defender.

**Xeno Guardian** is the prime example — costing only marginally more than pure soak while providing simultaneous blocking and damage output.

"I can't overemphasize enough how hard vigilant units counter threat. If there's vigilant defense in the set and your opponent is stacking up Frostbites, then go for the vigilant defense."

### Fake Threat (Part VI)
The breach warning system is conservative — it assumes opponents use all chill optimally, but this often overestimates actual threat. This creates **fake threat** when chill and blocker sizes don't match.

**Smaller blockers**: Chill 3 facing only 1-2 health blockers wastes freeze capacity. Subtract wasted chill from threat.
**Larger blockers**: Two Chill 3 units freezing a 6-health blocker wastes freeze. Calculate wasted freeze and reduce threat.

"If you avoid thinking about it and over-defend, then it will continue to be difficult...you won't learn much."

## Breach Theory
> Source: 307th — "What's in a Breach?" (prismatalibrary.blog, Dec 2017)

Four factors in a breach:

1. **Resource Liberation**: Frees up resources — no longer need to build defensive units. Particularly valuable when holding Drones and Steelsplitters purely for defense.
2. **Loss of Absorb**: Without your absorber, you take additional unsoaked damage each turn — a direct penalty.
3. **Vulnerable Unit Elimination**: "Your most vulnerable units are killed." Typically Tarsiers (1 HP) or Drones become targets.
4. **Opponent's Cost (Gambits)**: Sometimes opponents must sacrifice units to breach, creating gambit opportunities.

**Health value estimates** for evaluating breach outcomes: Wall ≈ 3 health, Drone ≈ 1.5, Shredder ≈ 4.5, 1 Gold ≈ 0.5 health.

**Breachproof strategies** focus on unit resilience, emphasizing high-health attackers. **Gambits** leverage the opponent's sacrifice costs to make the trade worthwhile.

## Short Term vs Long Term
> Source: 307th — "Short Term vs Long Term" (prismatalibrary.blog, Mar 2018)

Long-term units (Tarsier) persist throughout the game. Short-term units (Grimbotch) provide immediate value before dying.

**Five Decision Rules:**
1. **Pre-absorb phase**: Prioritize long-term units — short-term burst gets absorbed entirely.
2. **Cheap defense available**: Long-term units shine when "one point of Prompt defense costs around 2.2 Gold" — short-term damage loses value.
3. **High pressure**: Higher pressure favors short-term units. Rising damage forces increasingly inefficient enemy defense.
4. **Game duration**: Early games benefit from long-term investments; late games reward burst damage.
5. **Advanced units trump**: "Advanced set units are nearly always the better choice" over base-set alternatives, regardless of timing.

## Breachproof Strategy (Complete Framework)
> Source: 307th — Breachproof series (3 parts), prismatalibrary.blog, Apr–May 2018

### Part 1: Introduction
Breachproof is a strategy where players make their side "as resilient as possible, so that you can safely allow a breach" rather than preventing breaches at all costs. Standard strategies "build enough defense to avoid a breach every turn" — breachproof players "ignore the incoming damage and add on high health attackers instead."

**Mid-to-Late game divergence**: Standard player commits most resources to defense; breachproof player invests everything into attack. The tradeoff: "the standard player can use their attack to destroy the breachproof player's economy."

**End game**: When the breachproof player's economy is destroyed, they reach peak attack. "If the standard player can hold on and avoid a breach for a few more turns, they will be able to destroy the attackers and win."

**When it works**: Defensive supplies are limited, opponents commit to defense-weak strategies, strong breachproof attackers exist, or used reactively when defending becomes harder.

### Part 2: Types of Breachproof

**Pure Breachproof**: Converts Drones into breachproof economy using **Trinity Drone** or **Thorium Dynamo**. Uses breachproof attackers (Gauss Cannon, Iso Kronus, Tantalum Ray). Green pressure units (Cryo Ray, Nivo Charge) help. Weak against strong absorbers and soak.

**Semi Breachproof**: Operates without Trinity Drone/Thorium Dynamo. Enabled by **Wild Drone** ("5EE for 2 gold income is insanely efficient" + frontline breachproof) or **Gauss Fabricator**. "Easier to pull off" than pure variants.

**Venge All-in**: Converts Drones directly into breachproof attackers via Venge Cannon. "You want to save up a lot of Green and Drones and turn all of your Drones into Venge Cannons at once." **Cluster Bolt is essential** — "if Cluster Bolt isn't in the set, don't bother."

**Breachproof Transition**: Occurs late-game when "defending gets harder over time." Natural shift when standard strategies become unsustainable. Effectiveness depends on breach-vulnerability of current units — one-health attackers (Tarsiers) make transitions ineffective.

### Part 3: Rock, Paper, Scissors
"Rock paper scissors situations come up when there are three possible strategies that all counter each other."

The triangle: **Breachproof** beats standard play → **Standard style** (avoiding chill) beats breachproof → **Big Red/Tatsu** beats standard → **Breachproof** beats big red.

**Delayed commitment**: Players should "delay committing to a strategy indefinitely" by building economy, since the first player to commit gets countered. Eventually, resource constraints force "partial commitment" — moves toward one or two strategies while preserving flexibility.

**Shifting balance**: Big Red grows stronger as economies expand. Semi-breachproof deteriorates as more breach-vulnerable units accumulate. Pure breachproof maintains consistency.

## Granularity & Abuse Theory
> Source: Foxclear — Advanced Strategy Guide: Granularity and Abuse (wiki)

### What is Granularity?
"The ability to defend efficiently (with maximum absorb) no matter what amount of damage your opponent decides to cause." Example: 2 Walls against 3 attack = 3 damage on one Wall, no absorb on the other. Add an Engineer: 1 on Engineer, absorb 2 on Wall. Better granularity.

**Improving granularity**: Buy Engineers (optimal: 1-2 sitting around). But Engineers are not Prompt — must plan one turn ahead. A 2-health defender (Rhino, Forcefield) alongside an Engineer covers both 1 and 2 damage soak needs.

**Forcefield vs holding a Drone**: If you have an Engineer and need 1 more defense, Forcefield provides 1 more defense for the same cost (no Drone income lost). But if you need the green next turn, hold the Drone instead.

### Abusing Defenses
**Attack for 0**: Use units that spend attack (Bloodrager, Thermite Core, Lancetooth, Arka Sodara) or units that skip turns (Scorchilla, Iso Kronus). Against big absorbers, this denies absorb entirely. "Exceptionally well against 1-lifespan defenders like Plexo Cell."

**Granular attack**: Hold attackers (Steelsplitter, Militia) to hit specific numbers that deny absorb. Example: opponent has 2 Engineers + 2 Walls. Attacking for 5 = absorb 2 on Wall. But attacking for 4 = either absorb only 1 on Wall, or lose both Engineers and absorb 2 (leaving zero granularity next turn). **Always prefer the abuse if the held attacker has defensive value.**

**Freeze for granularity abuse**: Freeze Engineers with Cryo Ray or Iceblade Golem to remove granularity, then attack for the abusive number. "It is usually OK to spend multiple Cryo Ray charges to make even a 1-point abuse."

**Frontline weakness**: Click frontline units (Shredder, Iceblade Golem) to attack for exactly their HP. "Avoid having 2 Hannibulls at same time if your opponent has around 14 attack."

### Anti-Abuse Units
- **Engineer**: Core granularity unit. Optimal: 1-2 sitting around.
- **Innervi Field**: Grows when not attacked. But vulnerable if opponent can attack for 0 three turns in a row.
- **Rhino**: Prompt, gains threat/offensive value when opponent doesn't attack. Holding Rhino to cause abuse also saves a charge.
- **Cauterizer/Sentinel/Hellhound**: Produce Engineers.
- **Xaetron**: "The star against any kind of abuse." Heals on off-turns, gains health when attacked for 0, provides full defense on big turns.

### Fake Abuse Warning
"Having a granular attack is only worth if you get an advantage for not attacking for full value." If holding an attacker provides no defensive value, attack with it. Don't reduce damage for no reason.

### 1-Lifespan is NOT Granularity
Doomed Drones or Barriers reduce opponent's effective attack but don't help you absorb. If opponent can still abuse despite the lifespan soak, the soak is wasted.

## Gambit Theory (Complete Framework)
> Source: Foxclear — Advanced Strategy Guide: Gambits (wiki) + 307th Chill IV

### What is a Gambit?
"The decision to deliberately not defend fully when you have the ability to do so, and when you are not going breachproof." You bet that your opponent will not attack with full power next turn. **Different from ignoring fake threat** — all the threat you don't defend against is real.

### Why Gambit?
"Greed." You prefer to spend resources on something other than defense. Example: could hold a Drone to defend perfectly, but buy a Wall that defends for exact instead. "Often, the player feeling behind will be the one to gambit."

### Why NOT Take the Breach?
To accept a gambit, the opponent must commit defensive resources offensively (absorb on an attacker, click their absorber). This leaves them short on defense for YOUR next attack. "The immediate gain is not much — you offer a gambit for exact, so he will not kill much of your own threat."

### Easy Gambits (Safe to Offer)
**1-lifespan attackers** (Grimbotch, Chieftain, Doomed Mech) are easy to gambit against. Clicking them loses health AND defense tempo:
- 1 Grimbotch: opponent loses 2 health + 2 defense tempo. Must breach a Wall to break even.
- Chieftain: 7 health + 7 defense tempo. Very safe gambit.
- Doomed Mech: 5 health + can be absorber. Easy gambit.

**Aggressive gambits**: You can gambit for MORE than exact if opponent must keep a unit (e.g., Omega Splitter) regardless. Higher risk, higher reward.

### Dangerous Gambits
**Don't gambit a Centurion**: By taking the gambit, opponent removes threatening pressure while getting a breach. "It can work, but it will cost you a lot."

**Breachproof vulnerability**: If your attackers are breachproof (Gauss Cannons), gambiting is safer. If you have Tarsiers/Shadowfangs, breach kills high-value units.

### Should You TAKE a Gambit?
"Play your defense as if you are accepting the gambit. Then, try to defend against your opponent's next attack. If you end up in a worse position than what you gain from the gambit, restart and refuse it."

### Gambit Chaos
"Sometimes, to respond to a gambit, you can offer your own. When one is taken, it can quickly turn into chaos where both players offer a breach each turn." Usually means defense is desperate. Forcefield supply exhaustion can turn a good gambit into a bad one.

### Gambiting Units
Not just breaches — you can gambit individual units. "The most gambited one is the Rhino: basically, every time you buy a Rhino when your opponent has a threatening absorber, you actually gambit the Rhino against the absorber click."

## Reaction Theory — Reading Your Opponent
> Source: Foxclear — Advanced Strategy Guide: Reaction (wiki)

### Surprise Offensive
If you determined the set is high economy but your opponent goes aggressive (Fastimus, Elyot Animus): "forget about the 3rd Engineer and fall back on a lower economy. Don't take too long to retaliate after setting up your absorb."

Conversely, if opponent builds a 3rd Engineer when the set is aggressive: "go for a more offensive line to force your opponent to defend instead of expanding."

### The Perfect Threat
"Try to aim for the exact amount of threat that your opponent will not like to defend for." Example: opponent has 2 Engineers + Wall (5 defense). Attack for 8: a single Wall won't suffice, forcing expensive defense (Rhino, Forcefield, held Drone). Instant pressure units (Grimbotch, Fission Turret) are ideal for this.

### Anticipating Burst Damage
Count total attack opponent can send next turn (including new purchases). Count your total available defense next turn (including Prompt purchases). If short: buy preemptive defense a turn before.

### Disrupting Big Purchases
If opponent is setting up for a big unit (Amporilla, Vai Mauronax — usually requires double Animus or triple Blastforge): "see if you can get enough threat to stop him from purchasing his unit by massing damage. Sometimes, he will not be able to defend if he wants to buy it."

## Advanced Set Reading
> Source: Foxclear — Advanced Strategy Guide: Set Reading (wiki)

Beyond the basics, complex sets with many units require understanding strategic archetypes and how they interact:

### Strategic Archetypes
| Archetype | Beats | Loses To | Key Indicators |
|-----------|-------|----------|----------------|
| **Big Red** (RRR/RRRR units) | High economy | Aggression, burst, breachproof | Amporilla, Vai, Tatsu, Lucina, Shadowfang |
| **Aggressive** (low-cost attackers) | High economy, Big Red | Mid-tier absorb, burst, breachproof | Grimbotch, Fission Turret, cheap red |
| **Defensive** (big absorbers/soak) | Breachproof | Big Red, aggression | Centurion, Defense Grid, Polywall |
| **Breachproof** (high-HP attackers) | Big Red, aggression | High economy, defensive | Gauss Cannon, Iso Kronus, Trinity Drone |
| **Damage Sink** (absorb your own attack) | Good absorbers | High damage past sink capacity | Bloodrager, Thermite Core, Lancetooth |

### RPS Dynamics
"When the advanced set has a lot of units, it is not rare that multiple strategies are viable." The player who commits first gets countered. "It is advisable to wait until your opponent plays a decisive move toward one strategy, and then go for the counter."

### Counters & Synergies
"Some units absolutely counter others." Example: Shiver Yeti counters Plexo Cell. "Consider that the countered unit is not present in the set — but if your opponent can't access the counter, don't forget its existence."

Synergies: Valkyrion + Cryo Ray (Barriers from Valkyrion can be frozen). "Such synergies are strong: if you spot one, it should be a good idea to use it."

## Rush Timing Theory (Developer Framework)
> Source: Elyot Grant — "Balancing Prismata Openings Through Unit Design" Part 3

**Core insight**: "The optimal time to commit to a rush is as soon as it's not a losing play." This creates a dynamic where both players watch for the tipping point.

Five developer-verified facts about rush timing:
1. **Small economies**: Buying attackers first = losing play. Opponent absorbs damage, out-scales.
2. **Large economies**: Buying attackers first = winning play. Enough resources to both attack and defend.
3. **Later is better for rushers**: The longer the game goes, the more advantageous being first to attack.
4. **Rush commitment is irreversible**: Once you start attacking, you can't go back to economy.
5. **Commit as soon as it's not losing**: Wait too long and your opponent rushes first.

## Breach-Ambivalent Strategy
> Source: Developer design intent + community analysis

Distinct from breachproof: **breach-ambivalent** means building units that are OKAY with being breached but don't rely on it. Examples: Feral Warden (can block or attack), Protoplasm (high HP, can sacrifice), units with lifespan that have already generated value.

The key difference: breachproof EXPECTS breach and builds around it. Breach-ambivalent ACCEPTS breach as a possibility without building specifically for it.

## Endgame Theory
> Source: Developer design intent

The developers designed the endgame transition to be a "natural inflection point" where strategic priorities invert. Players who built long-term investments (Tarsiers, economy) suddenly face a world where short-term burst (Grimbotch, Tia Thurnax) becomes more valuable.

### Breach Targeting Priority
When breaching, optimal targeting order:
1. **Tarsiers** (1 HP, permanent attack — highest damage-per-health ratio)
2. **Drones** (1 HP, removes economy capacity)
3. **Animus** (removes 2 red/turn production)
4. **Blastforge** (removes 1 blue/turn production)
5. **Other tech buildings** and high-value units

## Economy Sizing Deep Dive
> Source: Foxclear — Beginner's Strategy Guide: Set Reading Basics (wiki) + 307th + community analysis

### Precise Drone Targets by Absorber
- **Wall** (3HP absorber): 12-15 Drones
- **Infusion Grid** (6HP): 16-19 Drones
- **Energy Matrix** (5HP, 2B): 18-22 Drones (high blue commitment)
- **Centurion** (6HP, 5 absorb): 20-25+ Drones (buy out all Drones possible)
- **Defense Grid** (10HP, 3B): 22-27 Drones (legendary, massive commitment)

### 26 vs 27 Drone Cap
Standard Drone supply is 21. With starting 6/7 Drones, max total is 27 for P1, 28 for P2. In practice, the "Drone cap" of ~26-27 is rarely reached because:
- Tech buildings cost Drone production turns
- Earlier attack commitment is usually optimal
- Supply of 21 limits maximum purchases
