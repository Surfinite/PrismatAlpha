# Category 6: Meta, Expert Opinions & Development History

## Development History
> Source: Wikipedia — Prismata

Development began in late 2010 by part-time MIT students. Originally a physical prototype called **MCDS** (Magic-Chess-Dominion-Starcraft).

"Prismata was created by Elyot Grant, Will Ma, Alex Fink, David Rhee, and Shalev Ben-David, who met as students and faculty at MIT."

The game launched on **Kickstarter** on October 7, 2014, raising $125,382 from 2,684 backers. Early Access on Steam began November 16, 2017.

## Developer Design Philosophy
> Source: Prismata Blog — "Balancing Prismata Openings Through Unit Design" series

### On First-Mover Advantage
"Player 1 has a small advantage in base-set-only Prismata, but in practice, many random sets end up favouring player 2."

Player 2's extra Drone enables exclusive openings. Lunarch addressed this through unit design rather than asymmetric rules.

### Balance Patch Approach (Jan 2019)
> Source: Prismata Blog — Balance Patch Post

**Philosophy**: "Rather than simply hitting obvious outliers with nerfs, we took a more nuanced approach — adjusting units that created undesirable patterns while also buffing units that were underused."

**Odin nerf (5HP → 3HP)**: "Odin is the most player-1-favouring unit in high-level Prismata play, a combination of its overall power... The decision to try a version of Odin with lower HP was actually inspired by a series of experiments."

**Wild Drone redesign**: "An alternate design for a Frontline economic unit in its place. This is really an entirely new unit with completely different synergies and opening strategy."

**Militia buff**: "Top Prismata player Msven championed this adjustment as a way of making the unit more dynamic and less boring."

**Redeemer**: "Some players suggested that this unit might favour player 2, but surprisingly, we found little to no bias toward player 2 in our statistics."

## Famous Set Analysis Examples
> Source: Set Reading Saturday series (307th, prismatalibrary.blog, Mar–Jun 2018)

Published analyses teach competitive set reading. 307th walks through the 6-step framework (identify absorber → economy size → tech → attackers → soak → opening) on real game sets.

### Inaugural Example (Mar 17)
**Set includes**: Xeno Guardian, Energy Matrix, Gauss Fabricator, Cryo Ray, Grenade Mech. **Strategy**: High economy (20 Drones) Energy Matrix build. "Three Blastforges and three Conduits, getting Gauss Fabricator first and Grenade Mechs and Cryo Rays later."

### A Set With No Absorber (Mar 24)
**Set**: Cryo Ray, Tantalum Ray, Immolite, Frost Brooder, Tesla Coil, Hannibull, Tatsu Nullifier, Amporilla. **Key constraint**: "There's no absorber in the random set" — must use Wall. Blue-Green (Tantalum Ray + Cryo Ray + Tesla Coil) beats Blue-Red. Opening: Slow Double Conduit (DD/DD/DCC).

### Tricky Blue-Green Set (Mar 31)
**Set**: Doomed Mech, Xeno Guardian, Nivo Charge, Amporilla, Fission Turret, Lancetooth, Venge Cannon, Scorchilla. **Key insight**: Nivo Charge counters Doomed Mech, making Xeno Guardian the only viable absorber. Reject red splashing — "a very hard time spending your Red later on." Backup Xeno Guardian prevents high-value Nivo Charge freezes.

### Breachproof? Or High Economy? (Apr 7)
**Set includes**: Defense Grid, Colossus, Trinity Drone, Asteri Cannon, Iceblade Golem, Galvani Drone. **RPS dynamic**: High Economy beats breachproof → Iceblade Breachproof counters high economy (freezes Defense Grid) → Asteri Cannon Breachproof defeats Iceblade variants. Recommended: Asteri Cannon breachproof initially, "focus on turning Drones into Trinity Drones first, and making attack later."

### Omega Splitter vs Protoplasm vs Arka Sodara (Apr 14)
**Three absorber candidates** with Shiver Yeti, Iceblade Golem, Nitrocybe. Omega requires 3 Blastforges creating Blue constraints and Chill vulnerability via Shiver Yeti. "A Protoplasm-centred strategy should be able to beat an Omega Splitter-centred strategy" despite lower raw power. Arka has "low impact on sets" because opponent just buys counter-Arka.

### Here Be Dragons (Apr 21)
**Set includes**: Vai Mauronax, Tia Thurnax, Blood Phage, Ossified Drone, Ferritin Sac, Doomed Wall. Vai "easily outvalues any high economy doomed wall centred strategy." Support units (Blood Phage, Ferritin Sac) "sink their Red and get out defense." Tia counter deemed insufficient. Opening: Fastimus into faster Vai.

### Centurion Plus A Whole Lot Of Red/Blue (Apr 28)
Economy target: "the low 20's" rather than typical 25+ for Centurion games — set has strong attack options. Tech path: CBBA. Unconventional opening prioritizes Animus over early Conduit for Tarsier production. Thermite Core "buff is too strong!" — denies 2 absorb/turn. 307th lost the test game.

### Lucina Timing (May 12)
**Set**: Energy Matrix, Lucina Spinos, Scorchilla, Bloodrager, Lancetooth, Nivo Charge. **Timing principle**: "The player with the natural timing should go for that as long as they're allowed to, while the player who doesn't have the natural timing should rush the powerful unit or combo uncomfortably quickly." P2 natural Lucina (15 Drones). P1 must rush (12 Drones, sacrifice efficiency for half-turn speed).

### A Legendary Set (Jun 9)
**Four legendaries**: Xaetron, Lucina Spinos, Endotherm Kit, Thunderhead, plus Feral Warden. Thunderhead ruled out — Xaetron "allows you to choose between waiting the Thunderhead out...or killing the Thunderhead with its click ability." Endotherm Kit soft-countered by both Xaetron and Feral Warden. Recommended: Red-Green Xaetron-into-Lucina with Feral Wardens as both attackers and defensive support.

## Game Design Insights (for commentary context)
> Source: Prismata Blog + Wikipedia

### Why No Randomness?
"Prismata was designed to eliminate luck entirely. Each game has a random set of units, but once the set is determined, all information is visible and all outcomes are deterministic."

### Why 8 Random Units?
The random set ensures no two games play identically while the base set ensures a stable strategic foundation. ~105 competitive units × 8 per game = enormous combinatorial variety.

### P1/P2 Balance Design
Rather than giving P1 and P2 identical starts, the designers gave P2 one extra Drone. This creates asymmetric opening theory — P2 has access to builds P1 cannot execute — while keeping overall win rates close to 50/50.

### Why the Game is Compared to StarCraft
"Turn-based StarCraft without a map." Economy management (Drones ≈ Workers), tech trees (Conduit/Blastforge/Animus ≈ tech buildings), army composition, timing attacks, macro vs micro. But pure strategy — no APM, no fog of war, no random crits.

## Competitive Meta Observations
> Source: Tips wiki + Standard Style + Common Openings

### Opening Book Study is Limited
"Unlike chess and some other games of complete information, opening book study is largely ineffective in Prismata due to the random unit set present in each game."

### Aggressive Builds Must Be Known
"Many aggressive builds are difficult or impossible to overcome if the opponent does not anticipate them and adjust accordingly. Familiarizing oneself with these builds is helpful in finding opportunities to use these tactics and, more importantly, learning to counter them."

### The Defender's Advantage is Real
Absorb creates a structural advantage for defenders. Attackers must overcome the absorb barrier before dealing meaningful damage. This is why economy-first (Standard Style) is the default competitive approach.

### When Aggression Wins
Per developer analysis: "When economies are large, buying attackers first is a winning play." The trick is identifying the right moment to switch from economy to attack — too early and you get out-scaled, too late and your opponent attacks first.

## Autowin Theory (Game Theory)
> Source: Yujiri — "Don't Hate Prismata for Every Set Being an Autowin" (yujiri.xyz, archived)

Every Prismata set is theoretically an autowin for one player. "You can assign a theoretical optimal-play victor to every possible gamestate, and therefore, even at the start of the game, there's always a player who can force a win." This applies to all deterministic turn-based games (Chess, Go, Checkers).

"But in most sets it only takes a very small mistake to make the theoretically-winning player lose to optimal play from the theoretically-losing player, and it has a high skill ceiling (ie. playing perfectly for an entire match is very difficult)."

**Commentary implication**: When noting P1/P2 advantage in a set, the AI can reference that while some sets structurally favor one player, execution matters far more than theory at any realistic level.

## New Player Learning Roadmap
> Source: RuinedShadows — "Master in a Month" (ruinedshadows.com, archived)

Structured milestone progression for new players reaching Master tier:

**Phase 1 (Week 1-2)**: Campaign Ch.1, Beginner/Intermediate Combat Training, read Standard Style (first pass), play Master Bot

**Phase 2 (Week 2-4)**: Advanced/Expert Combat Training, casual matches, beat Master Bot 30-50%, start ranked (~level 28-32)

**Phase 3 (Week 4-6)**: Watch 307th's Planning Ahead video, read Common Openings + Absorber Rankings + Intro to Set Reading. Focus on fundamentals: "making sure you absorb maximally, not overdefending, spending your resources efficiently"

**Phase 4 (Week 6-8)**: Learn set reading in depth. "Most of the time, the early turns determine the eventual outcome even if it isn't obvious at first." Questions to ask: What's the biggest absorber? What's the best damage? Are there synergies? How much econ do I need?

**Phase 5 (Master push)**: "The last couple tiers take the longest. Understanding gambits, counters, unit synergies, freeze tactics, granularity, and exploits."

**Key resources referenced**: 307th's articles (prismatalibrary.blog), Msven's YouTube series, amalloy's YouTube series (ep. 17+), top player Twitch streams

## Masterbot Exploitation Patterns
> Source: User-provided research (community analysis)

Understanding AI weaknesses provides commentary context for human vs AI games:

### 12-Drone Tarsier Rush
The Masterbot can be reliably beaten by rushing with fast Tarsiers and Walls at ~12 Drones economy. The bot over-values economy and responds too slowly to early aggression. "Not a legitimate strategy against humans, but reveals the bot's defensive timing weakness."

### Freeze/Lifespan Trap
Masterbot mishandles chill against lifespan units — it doesn't properly account for the interaction between freeze and lifespan timers, leading to wasted defensive resources.

### Vivid Drone Tempo Exploitation
Masterbot undervalues Vivid Drone's tempo advantage. A human player buying Vivid Drones early gains a resource lead that compounds over several turns.

### Breach Targeting Priority
When breaching, Masterbot targets: Tarsiers > Drones > Animus > Blastforge. This is generally correct but can be suboptimal in specific situations (e.g., killing a Blastforge may be more impactful than killing a Drone when the opponent needs blue for Wall defense).

## Militia Post-Buff Meta
> Source: User-provided research (Prismata Blog, Msven)

After the Militia buff (championed by top player Msven), Militia became significantly more dynamic. The buffed version provides:
- Click for 1 gold (economy option)
- Block with 2 HP (defensive option)
- Don't click for 1 attack (offensive option)

This triple-use nature makes Militia an excellent "finesse" unit — the opponent never knows which role it will fill on any given turn. Pre-buff, Militia was considered "boring" because optimal play was almost always to click for gold.

## Autowin Theory (Expanded)
> Source: Yujiri + User-provided research (community analysis)

### Specific Autowin Examples
While every set is theoretically an autowin for one player, certain unit combinations create near-unbeatable lines for one side:

- **Cluster Bolt + Venge Cannon**: P2 can set up an overwhelming Venge all-in that P1 struggles to answer
- **Flame Animus + Hannibull**: Creates an aggressive red+frontline strategy that's hard to defend efficiently
- **Gaussite Symbiote + Protoplasm**: Symbiote's burst combined with Protoplasm's scaling overwhelms most defenses

### Why Autowins Don't Matter in Practice
"In most sets it only takes a very small mistake to make the theoretically-winning player lose. Most alleged autowins have counters that aren't immediately obvious." — Yujiri

The community regularly identifies alleged "autowins" that turn out to have viable counter-strategies once players invest time exploring the set.

## Player Win Rate Statistics
> Source: Vendium Scrap design article, Lunarch Studios developer data

At 1800+ Elo:
- **P1 win rate**: 48.6%
- **P2 win rate**: 50.8%
- **Draw rate**: 0.6%

P2's extra Drone creates asymmetric opening theory. Some units (Odin) favor P1; others (Natural Conduit builds) favor P2. The developers used unit design (e.g., Vendium Scrap) to create more openings accessible to both players, narrowing the asymmetry.

## Yujiri's Emote/Ability Toggling
> Source: User-provided research (Yujiri's tips)

Skilled players can observe opponent's ability toggling and emote usage for information:
- Hovering over abilities reveals potential plays
- Toggle patterns can telegraph planned purchases
- Experienced players use deliberate misdirection

This is a form of "soft information" in a perfect-information game — the game state is fully visible, but the opponent's intentions are not.
