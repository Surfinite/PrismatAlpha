## **Double Blastforge Beats Reactive Defense**
### xYotsu (2231) vs Hey (2042) | `uP8mG-tr75d`
**Ranked | 20 turns | xYotsu wins**

**Set:** Innervi Field, Drake, Galvani Drone, Shadowfang, Doomed Mech, Mega Drone, Apollo, Vivid Drone

Two economy philosophies collide here. **xYotsu's blueprint**: aggressive early Blastforge doubling (T5) unlocks sustained tech diversity—Conduits for green flexibility, Galvani Drones for energy, Apollo for early aggression. Build *many* threats, adapt on the fly. **Hey's trap**: Galvani Drone at T5 alongside two Blastforges. Sound in isolation. But this early energy sink starves the economy exactly when it needed to scale *up*. By T6, xYotsu is buying Apollo + two Galvani Drones while Hey buys Apollo + Gauss Cannon—already struggling to match the unit count.

The set rewards infrastructure over desperation. **Blastforge** was the real MVP: enable blue mana for Walls and Forcefields, then pivot to whatever the board needs. **Innervi Field** looked flexible but became a noose—5 gold per play in a game where gold dried up.

### The Setup (T1–T7)

Both players mirrored the early drone greed. Then **T4–T5**: the fork.

xYotsu commits to **Blastforge, then Blastforge again + two Drones**. No hesitation. A 20-gold tech infrastructure in 10 gold of purchases—future-proofing.

Hey responds T5 with **Galvani Drone + Blastforge + Blastforge + Drones**. Reasonable on paper. But the Galvani Drone is premature. The economy size doesn't justify a 4-gold energy sink yet. Eval swings from even to xYotsu ahead.

T6–T7 compounds it. xYotsu accelerates: **Apollo + two Galvani Drones**, then **Steelsplitter + Drake**. Hey matches with Apollo and Gauss Cannon—but buying expensive single units while xYotsu buys *clusters* of synergy. By T7, xYotsu's board is wider and more threatening. Hey is already reacting instead of leading.

### The Turning Point (T8–T14)

**T11 is where it breaks.** xYotsu buys **Doomed Mech + Blastforge + two Conduits + Galvani Drone**. The infrastructure cascade completes. Those Conduits unlock green mana—more Forcefields, more defense, more flexibility. Hey buys **Doomed Mech + two Walls + Gauss Cannon + Galvani Drone**—defensive, reactive, expensive.

T12: The eval tightens momentarily (both "even"), but xYotsu is *generating* advantage, Hey is *spending* it.

**T13–T14 is the kill.** xYotsu goes **Wall + Innervi Field + Steelsplitter + Gauss Cannon + Engineers**, then **two Steelsplitters + two Gauss Cannons + two Blastforges + Engineers**. Massive threat density. Hey tries **Wall + Doomed Mech + Gauss Cannon** on T14—utterly outgunned. The eval gap explodes. xYotsu is dominant; Hey is even, then slipping.

By T14, Hey's economy is *broken*. She has the units but can't afford the next layer. xYotsu's Blastforge infrastructure keeps turning on. Game over.

### The Grind (T15–T20)

The final phase is a formality with teeth. Both players buy Doomed Mechs and Innervi Fields (they're the only tools left), but xYotsu's board is too dense. **T18** is surreal: xYotsu's eval *collapses* to losing—but that's mechanical noise. Hey buys Drake + Wall + Innervi Field. Still not enough attackers. xYotsu buys back and maintains redundancy.

**T20: Hey buys Wall + Innervi Field.** GG. The prompt defense could never answer the sheer volume of Steelsplitters, Gauss Cannons, and Doomed Mechs flooding in from turn 11 onward.

### What Decided It?

**Timing + infrastructure.** xYotsu read the set immediately: Blastforge is the engine. Double it early, expand into Conduits for mana flexibility, then spam whatever units the moment allows. Hey's Galvani Drone at T5 was 2–3 turns too early—it drained gold that should have funded *unit density*. By T11, xYotsu had built an economy that could sustain constant threat output. Hey was fighting with one hand tied behind her back, buying expensive defensive tech (Innervi Field) while the board already demanded bodies. Expensive defense beats cheap aggression. But cheap *infrastructure* beats expensive defense.
