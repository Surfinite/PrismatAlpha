## **xYotsu (2225) vs flopflop (2164) — A Comeback Built on Panic**

**Set:** Lucina Spinos, Redeemer, Tia Thurnax, Militia, Chieftain, Gauss Charge, Perforator, Thorium Dynamo, Ossified Drone

Both players opened greedy. **Thorium Dynamo** became the set's economy anchor—a breach-resistant unit that let both sides stack **Drones** without fear of early aggression. xYotsu stayed slightly ahead through T5, but at T6 everything cracked.

### **The Panic Buy That Lost T6**

xYotsu commits to **Tia Thurnax** plus *four Engineers*. It's a defensive over-commitment that screams miscalculation. The eval nosedives by 39 points—the largest single-turn swing of the game.

flopflop punishes immediately. **Forcefield**, **Rhino**, mixed **Engineers**, and a **Drone** (ply 12). It's textbook counterplay: defense *and* economy at once. A 50-point eval swing catapults flopflop into a crushing position. By T7, flopflop holds a near-insurmountable 50-point lead after another **Forcefield** buy.

### **Why This Matters**

xYotsu's four-Engineer panic buy violated the cardinal rule: when behind, scale offense or diversify tech. Instead, the approach was narrow and reactive. flopflop's response showed discipline—not overcommitting to attack, instead fortifying while keeping gold flowing. From T6 to T10, flopflop maintained a 20–24 point eval cushion with measured **Rhino**, **Tarsier**, and **Gauss Charge** purchases.

The game *should* have been over. But it wasn't.

### **Endgame Attrition: Where Evals Lie**

T10 is where flopflop stumbles. A **Gauss Cannon** + **Gauss Charge** buy generates a -23 eval swing—AI wanted **Animus**, **Drone**, and **Perforators** instead. The mistake isn't massive, but it's real: flopflop's positioning cracks just slightly.

xYotsu seizes the opening. T10 brings **Tarsier**, **Gauss Charge**, and **Rhino**—finally diversifying offense after rounds of Rhino trains. It's not a strategic masterclass, but it's enough to keep the game alive. T11 adds another **Rhino**.

The eval graphs suggest flopflop should win. He's been ahead for five straight rounds. He had the better midgame, the better positioning, the better resource allocation. Yet xYotsu emerges victorious at T11.

### **What Decided It?**

The likely culprit: **unit discipline in the grind**. flopflop's mixed-unit approach (Tarsier, Rhino, Gauss units) was more varied but also more scattered. xYotsu's obsessive **Rhino** focus, despite being born from panic, created a cohesive wall that absorbed incoming damage more efficiently. In a game without all-in bombs or Venge Cannon threats, the player with the tighter defensive shell and better-stacked absorb/breach units wins the attrition race.

flopflop won the midgame and lost the endgame. xYotsu panicked early, stabilized late, and executed the breakthrough when it mattered.
